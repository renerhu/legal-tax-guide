LLC vs S-Corp vs C-Corp: Which Business Structure Is Right for You?

📅 May 29, 2026 • 📖 11 min read • 🏷️ Small Business

Choosing the right business structure is one of the most consequential decisions you'll make as an entrepreneur. It affects your taxes, your personal liability, your ability to raise money, and how much paperwork you'll deal with. Here's everything you need to know to make the right choice.

⚠️ This guide provides educational information. Consult with a CPA or business attorney before making entity formation decisions.

1. The Big Picture

All three structures provide limited liability protection — meaning your personal assets (house, car, savings) are generally protected from business debts and lawsuits. The key differences are in taxation, compliance complexity, and ownership flexibility.

2. Limited Liability Company (LLC)

The LLC is the most popular structure for small businesses — and for good reason. It combines liability protection with simplicity and flexibility.

Advantages:

  • Pass-through taxation — Profits and losses flow directly to your personal tax return. No corporate tax layer.
  • Minimal paperwork — No annual meetings, board resolutions, or complex recordkeeping required
  • Flexible ownership — Can have one member or many; members can be individuals, entities, or foreign nationals
  • Lower compliance costs — Fewer ongoing requirements than corporations

Disadvantages:

  • Self-employment tax — All net income is subject to 15.3% SE tax (Social Security + Medicare)
  • Less attractive to investors — Venture capital firms prefer C-Corps
  • Varied state laws — LLC regulations differ significantly by state
💰 LLC sweet spot: Solopreneurs and small partnerships earning under $60K-80K/year in net profit. The simplicity outweighs the self-employment tax savings you'd get from an S-Corp.

3. S Corporation (S-Corp)

An S-Corp is not a separate entity type — it's a tax election you make after forming an LLC or corporation. You form an entity first, then file Form 2553 with the IRS.

How it saves you money:

With an LLC, you pay 15.3% self-employment tax on 100% of your net income. With an S-Corp, you pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll tax), and the remaining profits pass through without self-employment tax.

Example: $100,000 net profit

StructureSE TaxNet Savings
LLC$15,300 (on $100K)—
S-Corp ($60K salary)$9,180 (on $60K salary)$6,120 saved

Requirements for S-Corp election:

  • Must be a US-based corporation or LLC
  • Maximum 100 shareholders
  • Only one class of stock
  • Shareholders must be US citizens or residents
  • Must file Form 2553 by March 15 of the election year

Ongoing responsibilities:

  • Run payroll (weekly, biweekly, or monthly)
  • File quarterly payroll tax returns (Form 941)
  • Issue W-2 to yourself
  • Hold annual board meetings
  • File Form 1120-S annually
⚠️ Warning: The IRS requires you to pay yourself a "reasonable salary." Setting salary too low to avoid taxes is a well-known audit trigger. For most professions, reasonable salary is 40-60% of net profit.

4. C Corporation (C-Corp)

C-Corps are the default corporate structure and the choice of most publicly traded companies. They're more complex but offer unique advantages.

Advantages:

  • Raising capital — Can issue multiple classes of stock, attract venture capital, and go public
  • Employee benefits — Can deduct health insurance, retirement plans, and other benefits more favorably
  • Unlimited ownership — No restrictions on who can own shares (international investors, other corporations)
  • Perpetual existence — Continues regardless of ownership changes

Disadvantages:

  • Double taxation — Corporate profits are taxed at 21% (federal), then dividends are taxed again on your personal return
  • Significant paperwork — Annual meetings, board resolutions, minutes, state filings, separate tax return
  • Higher formation and maintenance costs — Expect $1000+/year in compliance costs
💡 C-Corp reality check: Unless you're raising venture capital or planning an IPO, you probably don't need a C-Corp. The double taxation and compliance burden outweigh the benefits for most small businesses.

6. Tax Implications Deep Dive

LLC taxes: File Schedule C (single-member) or Form 1065 (multi-member) alongside your personal 1040. You'll pay income tax + 15.3% self-employment tax on all net earnings.

S-Corp taxes: File Form 1120-S annually. You'll pay payroll taxes on your salary, but distributions beyond your salary are tax-free (not subject to SE tax).

C-Corp taxes: File Form 1120. The corporation pays 21% federal tax on profits. Distributions to shareholders (dividends) are taxed again at capital gains rates (0-20%).

7. How to Decide

Start with an LLC if:

  • You're a solo entrepreneur or freelancer
  • Your net profit is under $60K/year
  • You value simplicity over tax optimization
  • You might have international business partners

Elect S-Corp status when:

  • Your net profit exceeds $60K-80K consistently
  • You're willing to run payroll and handle compliance
  • You're a US citizen/resident (S-Corp requires this)
  • You want to reduce self-employment tax

Choose C-Corp if:

  • You're raising venture capital or angel investment
  • You plan to go public someday
  • You need to issue stock options to employees
  • You have international investors
📊 The most common path: Start as a sole proprietor → Form an LLC at $30K+ income → Elect S-Corp status at $60K+ profit → Convert to C-Corp only if you raise institutional funding.

8. Can You Change Later?

Yes, and it's common. You can:

  • Convert a sole proprietorship to an LLC at any time (non-taxable event in most states)
  • Elect S-Corp status for your LLC by filing Form 2553
  • Convert an LLC to a C-Corp (common when raising venture capital)
  • Convert C-Corp to S-Corp under certain conditions

You're not locked into your initial choice. But changing structures does involve paperwork and costs, so starting with the simplest option that meets your current needs is usually the smartest approach.
