Self-Employment Tax 2026: Complete Guide for Freelancers & Independent Contractors

📅 May 30, 2026 • 📖 10 min read • 🏷️ Tax Guides

If you're self-employed — freelancer, independent contractor, sole proprietor, or gig worker — you've probably noticed something about your tax bill: it's higher than you expected. That's because you pay both the employee and employer portions of Social Security and Medicare taxes.

This is called self-employment tax, and it's one of the most significant tax obligations for anyone who works for themselves. In 2026, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) introduced changes that affect how much you owe and what you can deduct.

This guide covers everything you need to know — the rates, how to calculate what you owe, strategies to reduce your tax burden, and how to file correctly.

What Is Self-Employment Tax?

Self-employment tax is the self-employed equivalent of the payroll taxes that W-2 employees and their employers split. It consists of two parts:

  • Social Security (12.4%) — Funds retirement, disability, and survivor benefits
  • Medicare (2.9%) — Funds hospital insurance coverage

Total rate: 15.3% of your net self-employment income.

For comparison, a W-2 employee pays 7.65% (half), and their employer pays the other 7.65%. When you're self-employed, you pay both halves — that's the "self-employment tax penalty" you've heard about.

However, you can deduct the employer-equivalent portion (7.65%) on your income tax return, which reduces your overall income tax burden. We'll cover this in detail below.

Self-Employment Tax Rates for 2026

The 2026 rates remain at the standard 15.3% but with updated wage bases:

Tax Component Rate 2026 Wage Base
Social Security (employer + employee) 12.4% $176,100 (estimated)
Medicare (employer + employee) 2.9% No cap (all net earnings)
Total SE Tax 15.3% —

Important: The Social Security wage base increases annually with inflation. For 2026, it's estimated at $176,100 (up from $168,600 in 2024). Earnings above this threshold are not subject to the Social Security portion but still face the 2.9% Medicare tax.

Additional Medicare Tax: If your self-employment income exceeds $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly), you owe an extra 0.9% on earnings above those thresholds. This brings the total Medicare rate to 3.8% for high earners.

Who Needs to Pay Self-Employment Tax?

You must pay self-employment tax if your net self-employment earnings are $400 or more in a tax year. This includes income from:

  • Freelance work (writing, design, consulting, programming)
  • Independent contracting (Uber, DoorDash, Upwork, Fiverr)
  • Running a sole proprietorship or single-member LLC
  • Partnership income
  • Direct sales and gig economy work

Even if you have a full-time W-2 job, you must pay SE tax on your side hustle income if it exceeds $400 net.

💡 Key threshold: If you earn less than $400 net from self-employment, you don't owe SE tax (but you may still need to report the income).

How to Calculate Self-Employment Tax

Here's the step-by-step process for calculating what you owe:

Step 1: Calculate Net Self-Employment Income

Start with your gross self-employment income and subtract your allowable business expenses. This is your net profit (or loss) from Schedule C.

Formula: Gross Income − Business Expenses = Net Self-Employment Income

Step 2: Apply the 92.35% Factor

The IRS allows you to exclude 7.65% (the employer-equivalent portion) before calculating the SE tax. So you multiply your net earnings by 92.35% to get your net earnings subject to SE tax.

Formula: Net Self-Employment Income × 92.35% = Net Earnings Subject to SE Tax

Step 3: Apply the 15.3% Rate

Apply 12.4% for Social Security (up to the wage base) and 2.9% for Medicare (uncapped).

Example Calculation

Let's say you're a freelance web developer earning $80,000 net profit in 2026:

Line Item Amount
Net self-employment income (Schedule C) $80,000
Multiply by 92.35% $73,880
Social Security (12.4% × $73,880) $9,161
Medicare (2.9% × $73,880) $2,143
Total SE Tax $11,304

That's $11,304 you owe on top of your regular income tax. But you get to deduct half of this — more on that next.

The Self-Employment Tax Deduction

The IRS lets you deduct the employer-equivalent portion (7.65%) of your SE tax as an above-the-line adjustment to income. This means you don't need to itemize to claim it.

In the example above, you'd deduct $5,652 (half of $11,304) from your adjusted gross income. This doesn't reduce your SE tax itself — it reduces your income tax liability.

Think of it this way: the government recognizes it's unfair that you pay both halves, so they let you deduct the "employer" half from your income taxes.

2026 Changes Under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)

The OBBBA, signed into law in early 2026, brought several changes that affect self-employed taxpayers:

1. QBI Deduction at 23%

The Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction, also known as Section 199A, is set at 23% for tax year 2026. This means you can deduct up to 23% of your qualified business income from your taxable income — potentially saving thousands. This deduction is available to most sole proprietors, LLCs, and S-corporations below certain income thresholds.

2. Section 179 Limits Increased

Equipment and asset purchases can be expensed up to $2.56 million in 2026, making it easier to reduce your net self-employment income (and therefore your SE tax) through legitimate business investments.

3. 100% Bonus Depreciation

Qualified business assets placed in service in 2026 are eligible for 100% bonus depreciation, allowing you to write off the full cost in the first year.

Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

Unlike W-2 employees who have taxes withheld from each paycheck, self-employed individuals must pay estimated taxes quarterly. This includes both income tax and self-employment tax.

2026 Estimated Tax Payment Deadlines

Payment Period Due Date
January 1 – March 31 April 15, 2026
April 1 – May 31 June 15, 2026
June 1 – August 31 September 15, 2026
September 1 – December 31 January 15, 2027

You can use Form 1040-ES to calculate and make these payments online via IRS Direct Pay. If you underpay or miss a payment, you could face an underpayment penalty even if you pay the full amount at tax time.

How to File Self-Employment Tax

Filing self-employment tax requires two forms in addition to your standard 1040:

Schedule C (Form 1040): Profit or Loss From Business

This is where you report your self-employment income and deduct your business expenses. The net profit figure flows to Schedule SE.

Schedule SE (Form 1040): Self-Employment Tax

This form calculates your actual SE tax liability. It applies the 92.35% factor and the 15.3% rate, then tells you how much to deduct as the employer-equivalent portion.

💡 Pro Tip: Use tax software. Most platforms (TurboTax Self-Employed, H&R Block, FreeTaxUSA) handle Schedule C and Schedule SE automatically. The forms are complex enough that manual filing is error-prone for first-timers.

Strategies to Reduce Your Self-Employment Tax

You can't avoid SE tax entirely if you have self-employment income, but you can reduce it through legitimate strategies:

1. Maximize Business Deductions

Every dollar of business expense reduces your net self-employment income, which directly reduces your SE tax. Don't miss:

  • Home office deduction (simplified or regular method)
  • Health insurance premiums (deducted on Schedule 1)
  • Retirement contributions (SEP IRA, Solo 401(k))
  • Vehicle expenses (standard mileage or actual cost)
  • Equipment and software (Section 179 or bonus depreciation)
  • Professional services (accounting, legal, consulting)

2. Contribute to a Retirement Plan

Contributions to a SEP IRA or Solo 401(k) reduce your adjusted gross income. For 2026, SEP IRA contributions can be up to 25% of your net earnings (capped at $70,000). A Solo 401(k) allows even higher contributions if you're under 50.

3. Consider an S-Corp Election

Once your net income exceeds roughly $60,000–$80,000, electing S-corp status can reduce SE tax. As an S-corp, you pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll tax) and take the rest as distributions (not subject to SE tax). This is a complex strategy — consult a CPA before pursuing it. For a deeper comparison, see our guide on LLC vs S-Corp vs C-Corp.

4. Time Your Income and Expenses

If you expect lower income next year, defer sending invoices until January. If you expect higher income, accelerate expenses by purchasing needed equipment before year-end.

Self-Employment Tax vs. Income Tax: What's the Difference?

Many freelancers confuse these two, but they're separate obligations:

Income Tax Self-Employment Tax
What it funds General government operations Social Security + Medicare
Rate 10%–37% (progressive brackets) 15.3% flat (on qualifying income)
Standard deduction applies ✅ Yes ($15,000 single, 2026 est.) ❌ No
Reduced by business expenses ✅ Yes ✅ Yes (indirectly, via net income)
Quarterly payments required ✅ Yes ✅ Yes (combined with income tax)

You pay both — and the combined bill can be significant. A freelancer earning $80,000 net might owe $11,304 in SE tax plus roughly $12,000–$14,000 in federal income tax. That's nearly a third of their income going to taxes.

Common Self-Employment Tax Mistakes

Avoid these pitfalls that trip up many first-time freelancers:

  • Not paying quarterly: The biggest mistake. You don't need to wait until April. Missing quarterly payments triggers penalties.
  • Forgetting the SE tax deduction: Half of your SE tax is deductible above the line — don't leave this on the table.
  • Confusing gross and net income: You pay SE tax on net profit, not gross revenue. Track every eligible expense.
  • Ignoring state taxes: Most states also require self-employed individuals to pay state income and sometimes state-level SE taxes.
  • Not tracking expenses throughout the year: Trying to reconstruct a year of expenses in March is painful and error-prone.

Final Thoughts

Self-employment tax is a significant obligation — 15.3% on top of your income tax — but it's manageable with proper planning. The key takeaways:

  • Pay estimated taxes quarterly to avoid penalties
  • Maximize legitimate business deductions to reduce net income
  • Contribute to a retirement plan to lower your AGI
  • Claim the employer-equivalent SE tax deduction on your 1040
  • Consider S-corp election if your income exceeds $80,000

Remember: paying SE tax isn't just a burden — it builds your Social Security and Medicare eligibility. Every dollar you contribute increases your future retirement benefits. In that sense, it's an investment in your future self.
