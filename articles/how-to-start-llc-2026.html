How to Start an LLC in 2026: The Complete Step-by-Step Guide

📅 May 29, 2026 • 📖 12 min read • 🏷️ LLC Formation

Forming a Limited Liability Company (LLC) is one of the smartest moves a small business owner or freelancer can make. It protects your personal assets, offers tax flexibility, and takes less than a week in most states.

Here's exactly how to do it in 2026 — from choosing your state to filing the paperwork, getting your EIN, and staying compliant.

⚠️ Important: This guide provides general educational information. Requirements vary by state and business type. Consult a licensed attorney for advice specific to your situation.

What Is an LLC?

An LLC is a legal business structure that combines the liability protection of a corporation with the tax simplicity of a sole proprietorship. If your LLC gets sued, your personal assets (house, car, savings) are generally protected.

Step 1: Choose Your State

Most people assume they should form their LLC in Delaware or Wyoming. But for most small businesses, the best state is the one where you physically operate.

Scenario Recommended State Why
Single-member LLC, local business Your home state Simplest, lowest cost, no extra compliance
Online business, national clients Your home state Foreign registration in home state still required if you operate there
Venture-funded startup Delaware Investor-friendly laws, but you'll need to register in your home state too
Single-member LLC, privacy-focused Wyoming or New Mexico Wyoming: no state income tax; New Mexico: no public owner disclosure

Step 2: Choose a Business Name

Your LLC name must be unique within your state. Check your Secretary of State's business name database before filing.

Naming rules:

  • Must include "Limited Liability Company," "LLC," or "L.L.C."
  • Cannot include restricted words (bank, insurance, etc.) without special licenses
  • Must not be confusingly similar to an existing business name

Pro tip: Reserve your domain name (yourbusiness.com) and social media handles before filing — even if you're not building a website yet.

Step 3: File Articles of Organization

This is the official paperwork. You file it with your state's Secretary of State office (or equivalent).

What you'll need:

  • LLC name and address
  • Registered agent information
  • Management structure (member-managed vs manager-managed)
  • Filing fee (typically $50–500 depending on state)

Filing options:

  • DIY online — File directly through your state's website. Cheapest option ($50-200 usually)
  • Registered agent service — LegalZoom, ZenBusiness, Northwest (adds $0-299 on top of state fees)

Step 4: Appoint a Registered Agent

A registered agent receives legal documents on your behalf. Requirements:

  • Must have a physical address in the state of formation
  • Must be available during business hours
  • Can be you, a friend, or a professional service

If you work from home, you can be your own registered agent. If privacy matters, use a service like Northwest Registered Agent ($125/year).

Step 5: Get an EIN (Employer Identification Number)

An EIN is your business's Social Security number. You need it to open a bank account, file taxes, and hire employees.

Best part: It's completely free from the IRS. Apply online at IRS.gov — approval is instant, and you get your EIN immediately.

Single-member LLCs: You can use your SSN instead of an EIN, but getting one anyway keeps your personal and business finances separate.

Step 6: Create an Operating Agreement

An operating agreement outlines how your LLC is run — ownership percentages, profit distribution, voting rights, and management structure.

Myth: "I don't need one since I'm the only owner."
Reality: Banks may require one to open accounts. Some states (California, New York) legally require it. And it protects your liability shield in court.

Free templates are available online, or you can create one with AI assistance in 15 minutes.

Step 7: Open a Business Bank Account

Once you have your EIN and operating agreement, open a dedicated business checking account. This is critical for maintaining your liability protection — mixing personal and business funds is the #1 way LLC owners get their liability shield pierced.

Step 8: Check Licenses & Permits

Depending on your industry and location, you may need additional business licenses:

  • General business license (city/county level)
  • Professional license (real estate, contractor, etc.)
  • Sales tax permit (if selling products)
  • Home occupation permit (if running from home)

Ongoing Compliance (Don't Skip This)

An LLC requires annual maintenance:

Requirement Frequency Typical Cost
Annual Report Yearly $0–800 (CA is expensive)
State Franchise Tax Yearly $0–800+ (varies by state)
Registered Agent Yearly $0–300
Business License Renewal Yearly $50–400

Should You Use an LLC Service?

For most single-member LLCs, DIY filing is perfectly fine. The state websites are straightforward.

Use a service if: you value privacy (they'll be your registered agent), you want to form in a different state, or you're setting up a multi-member LLC and want everything handled.

Quick Summary

  1. Choose your state — usually your home state
  2. Pick a unique name
  3. File Articles of Organization online ($50–520)
  4. Get an EIN from the IRS (free, instant)
  5. Write an operating agreement
  6. Open a business bank account
  7. Stay on top of annual compliance

Forming an LLC usually takes 2–5 business days (expedited filing available in most states for an extra fee). Congratulations — you're now a business owner!
